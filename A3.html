Phase One: Sketching Visualizations

Question: What shifts have occurred in the distribution of the population from 1900 to 2000, and do these trends differ between the sexes?

Design Rationale for Sketch 1:

  • The aim was to create a clear snapshot visualization illustrating the distribution of male and female populations across age groups in both censuses. By stacking horizontal bars in age group order, viewers can easily discern the distribution pattern depicted by the bar graph's shape. The symmetrical layout facilitates direct comparisons between genders.
  • The goal was to highlight differences in distributions between the two censuses and identify any distinct trends between genders. While changes in magnitude are readily visible, it's challenging to discern differences, if any, in how male and female populations evolved from 1900 to 2000.
  • An aspect requiring clarification in future iterations is the overlap of bars. Despite color/pattern differentiation, it remains unclear whether the 2000 bar includes counts from 1900 or represents a separate count starting where the 1900 bar ends. This issue should be addressed in future iterations for enhanced clarity.

Design Rationale for Sketch 2:

  • The objective here was to provide comprehensive insights into population dynamics from 1900 to 2000 by presenting both absolute population numbers for each year and the relative change in each age group for the two sexes.
  • The aim was not only to illustrate the overall population increase trend but also to highlight differences in the relative increase of specific age group populations between 1900 and 2000, particularly between genders. Notably, female populations in older age groups experienced a significantly larger increase (4000%) compared to older male populations (2000%). This was effectively demonstrated by the fixed axis ranges, enabling direct comparison of line plots and showing a substantial upward trend in the female line plot towards the right compared to the male line plot.
  • However, it may be beneficial to consider using a log-scale axis for the percentage increase, especially for older age groups where the percentage increase is notably high compared to the younger age groups. Additionally, to avoid confusion with two axes, it's important to provide additional information such as color coordination to ensure that the correct elements of the graph are associated with the correct axis.

Design Rationale for Sketch 3:

  • I believed a pie chart would effectively depict the male and female population proportions relative to the total population, and whether the percentage composition of specific age groups for each sex changed over time.
  • My intention was for the visualization to indicate the dominant sex in terms of population in both 1900 and 2000, as well as to illustrate the predominant age group within the population and whether this composition shifted over the years.
  • While the pie chart successfully conveyed information about the sex ratio by dividing it into male and female segments, discerning the relative population increase between 1900 and 2000 from pie sizes alone proved challenging. Furthermore, drawing meaningful conclusions about changes in distribution from slice sizes was difficult and required additional inference from the viewer.

Reflection

I find that each of the three sketches has its own set of strengths and weaknesses. The first sketch effectively conveys the population distribution across age groups for both 1900 and 2000, yet it lacks details regarding the relative population changes within each age group, which are crucial but not easily discernible from the visualization alone. On the other hand, the second graph is the most informative, successfully illustrating the relative changes across age groups while also providing information about the absolute population. However, the dual axis arrangement can be confusing, and I believe there is room for improvement in scaling the axis for percentage increase. As for the final sketch, it offers unique insights by presenting age/sex groups relative to the total population. However, it falls short in providing adequate details to gauge changes in distribution accurately. Moving forward, I intend to blend elements from the first two sketches in my visualization. I plan to base it on the first sketch but incorporate the relative increase information from the second sketch to enhance its informativeness. Additionally, I will consider modifying elements in the 2nd plot in the relative increase axis, such as employing log-scaling for the axis, to improve clarity. Although I do not plan on incorporating key elements of sketch 3, I do appreciate the use of size as a design element in the pie charts, and may integrate this concept to differentiate between 1900 and 2000 bars in my final visualization.

Phase Two: Final Design

Design Process & Design Decisions

What story am I trying to tell?
Following the sketching and reflection process, I proceeded to synthesize my final visualization by incorporating elements from my sketches to enhance it. Opting for the vertical bar graph, I found it to be the most effective in conveying the overall population distribution at a glance, providing digestible information to the viewer. However, I decided to augment this population graph by including an additional element: the magnitude of relative population change across the two years for each age group. With this addition, the aspects of the data I am trying to most effectively communicate are twofold: 1) to highlight the substantial increase in population over the century for both genders and 2) illustrating the notable difference in population increase between older age groups of males and females. These two narratives are also two of the most prominent aspects of my visualization, evident in the considerable difference in length between the 1900 and 2000 population bars, and the asymmetry in the length of the % Increase bars towards the bottom of the graph.

Visual encodings I used and the rationale
I selected the vertical bar graph, a widely recognized visualization method for population data. I believe its familiarity to most audience members allows them to immediately grasp the emphasized visual elements without needing to decipher the main graph or axis. As well, I like the symmetric aspect of this visualization as it aids in easy observation of changes without additional effort. I sorted the graph by age groups and positioned the age group labels in the center to prevent them from being obscured and to maintain symmetry in the visualization. To distinguish between populations from different centuries, I used different colors and sizes for the inner bar graph. This makes it clear that the data represents two distinct time periods, and adjusting the size of bars to show overlap prevents confusion about whether the data is cumulative. For the outer bar graph, I used a different color for the bar to distinguish it from the inner graph. For both inner and outer graphs, I made sure to keep the axis range consistent for male and female sizes to allow for easy comparison. To simplify the visualization, I combined age groups into 10-year increments instead of 5-year increments, as I felt that although 5-year increments were fine-grained and informative, it felt overwhelming especially with the double bar-graph formation. I also calculated the relative percentage increase to include in the visualization. While I experimented with logarithmic plotting for conveying this information, I found that it obscured the difference in relative increase between the two sides. Therefore, I opted for a linear scale, similar to the visualization in sketch 2.

Limitations of the Visualization
Some aspects downplayed or obscured by my visualization design include the overall population makeup and the magnitude of older population groups. For instance, it's unclear whether there's a larger female or male population overall, and whether this changes over the two decades. Additionally, precise magnitude information for older population groups is obscured as the magnitudes decrease significantly, making it difficult to gauge for both 2000 and 1900. However, this information is supplemented by the percentage increase data, which offers insights into older populations that may be hard to discern visually.

Reflection

Sketching was definitely beneficial for me, and I shaped most of my major design choices based on elements from one of the three sketches in phase 1. By sketching each design and evaluating their strengths and weaknesses, I developed an enhanced visualization that leveraged the best features of multiple sketches.For example, although I liked the graph for sketch 1, I found it less informative compared to sketch 2, which I addressed in my final visualization by adding an additional axis inspired by sketch 2. Additionally, I refined or discarded confusing elements from sketch 1, such as adjusting axis labels and distinguishing between bars for 1900 and 2000. This iterative process saved time and allowed me to identify areas for improvement in future iterations before proceeding with creating the final visualization.
